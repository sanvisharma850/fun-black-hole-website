01 — What We Know

The physics of the
impossible made visible

Horizons define the limits of what is knowable. In astrophysics, the event horizon is that limit made absolute — a boundary beyond which no signal, no matter, no photon can ever return to inform us of what lies within. It is the universe's most radical form of privacy.

Across the observable universe, roughly one in every thousand galaxies harbors a supermassive black hole at its center — an object so gravitationally dominant that even light itself cannot escape once it crosses the boundary. M87*, six and a half billion solar masses, was the first we ever photographed.

Relativistic time dilation near a black hole is not metaphor — it is measurable physics. A clock placed close to the event horizon ticks measurably slower than one in open space. At the horizon itself, time as we experience it simply… stops. From outside, an infalling object freezes forever at the edge.

Spacetime is not a fixed backdrop. It is a dynamic fabric woven by mass and energy — curved, stretched, warped. Einstein's field equations describe how matter tells space how to bend, and space tells matter how to move. Every orbit, every photon path, every gravitational wave is a sentence in that language.

Hawking radiation, proposed in 1974, suggests black holes evaporate. Quantum fluctuations at the event horizon produce particle pairs — one escapes, one falls in. Over timescales that dwarf the current age of the universe, the black hole loses mass, degree by imperceptible degree, until nothing remains.

Inside the singularity, every known law of physics unravels. Infinite density, zero volume, the collapse of all predictive models. It is not merely the end of space — it may be the end of causality itself. What happens there exists outside the reach of any equation we have ever written.

The Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black hole's shadow in 2019 — the dark silhouette cast against the glowing infalling disk. That image, assembled from telescopes across the entire Earth, is the closest we can ever see to absolute nothingness. And it is strangely, unexpectedly beautiful.

02 — Classification

Kerr, Schwarzschild,
and beyond

Not all black holes are the same. A Schwarzschild black hole has mass and nothing else — perfectly spherical, rotationally inert. A Kerr black hole rotates, dragging spacetime itself in frame-dragging.

The Kerr metric introduces an ergosphere — a region where nothing can remain stationary. Energy can be extracted from this region via the Penrose process: the only known way to harvest a black hole's rotational energy.

Some gravitational systems exhibit persistent orbital capture beyond expected decay models. What governs these anomalous binding relationships remains, for now, an open question at the edge of known physics.
